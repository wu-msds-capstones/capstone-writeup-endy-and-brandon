Introduction

For seasoned real estate professionals, the ability to accurately estimate a property’s market value and time on the market is a cornerstone of their expertise. This intuition, developed over years of experience, provides a significant advantage in a competitive landscape. However, for newcomers,be it first-time homebuyers navigating a complex process or new agents striving to build a successful career, this knowledge is not readily available. This project aims to bridge that knowledge gap within Oregon’s dynamic housing market by leveraging data science to uncover the key drivers of property valuation.

The central research question guiding this project is: Which quantifiable property characteristics in Oregon are the most significant predictors of a residential real estate property’s final sale price and its time on the market?. While this paper will focus on predicting the final sale price, the groundwork is laid for a future survival analysis to address the time-on-market component.

Background

The Oregon housing market provides a compelling case study. In the last year, the state has seen a 0.3% rise in median home prices and a 7.9% increase in housing supply (Redfin 2025). Yet, the median days on the market has also increased, suggesting a potential shift in market dynamics. By building a predictive model based on a robust set of property features, we can distill these complex market signals into actionable insights, making the market more transparent and navigable for everyone involved.

Key metrics for evaluating the market include the Case-Shiller Index, which tracks home value, and Days on the Market (DOM), the time a property is listed before selling (S&P Dow Jones Indices 2025). Our research seeks to identify the property characteristics that are the most significant predictors of both a property’s final sale price and its DOM.

Methodology

Our approach combines several data science disciplines to create a comprehensive analysis, from data collection to predictive modeling.

Data Engineering & Acquisition

The primary data was sourced from the RealEstateAPI, a comprehensive platform offering detailed information across more than 19 distinct property-related tables (RealEstateAPI 2025b, 2025a). A proportional sampling strategy was employed to collect data on 50,000 unique properties, with the number of properties from each Oregon county weighted by its proportion of the state’s total population. The raw, multi-table data required significant processing. Tables were joined on the common property_id key to create a single analytical dataset. This initial dataset contained 77,922 records but revealed missing values in critical columns. For this baseline analysis, we handled this missing data by dropping all rows containing any null values, resulting in a final, complete dataset of 34,507 records. While this method ensures data integrity, we acknowledge its limitations.

Statistical Thinking & Machine Learning

To understand the data and build a predictive tool, we implemented two forms of regression analysis:

  1. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Regression: Using the statsmodels library, we conducted an OLS analysis to rigorously test which features have a statistically significant impact on sale price and to diagnose potential statistical issues like multicollinearity.
  2. Predictive Linear Regression: Using scikit-learn, we trained a linear regression model to predict final sale prices. The dataset was split into an 80% training set and a 20% testing set to provide an unbiased evaluation of the model’s performance on new, unseen data. The model’s performance was evaluated using R-squared (\(R^2\)) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE).

Results

The machine learning model yielded strong baseline results for predicting property sale prices.

  • Model Performance: On the unseen test data, our model achieved an R-squared (\(R^2\)) of 0.730. This indicates that our model can explain approximately 73% of the variance in property sale prices. The Mean Absolute Error (MAE) was $92,834. This means that, on average, the model’s prediction is off by about $92,834.

  • Feature Importance: As shown in the visualization of the model’s coefficients (Figure 4 (a)), the most impactful positive predictor was whether the purchase was financed. Transaction types, such as construction sales and subdivision transfers, also strongly increased the predicted price. The most impactful negative predictors included having RV parking and a balcony.

  • Statistical Findings: The OLS regression analysis revealed a high Condition Number of 9.97e+07, a strong indicator of multicollinearity. This suggests that while the model as a whole is predictive, the specific coefficient for any single feature may be unstable.

Conclusions & Future Work

This research successfully developed a baseline model for predicting real estate sale prices in Oregon, identifying key value drivers and achieving an R² of 0.73. Our analysis confirmed that physical characteristics like living area, as well as transactional details like the purchase method, are significant predictors of a property’s final sale price.

However, the analysis also highlighted significant limitations that provide a clear path for future work. Our statistical tests revealed strong multicollinearity in the feature set, which makes the specific impact of any single feature difficult to isolate. Furthermore, our decision to drop records with missing data, while expedient, resulted in the loss of over half our initial data and may have introduced sampling bias.

To build upon this foundation and create a more robust and reliable tool, the next steps are clear:

  1. Improve Data Integrity: Implement sophisticated imputation techniques to handle missing values, thereby retaining a larger and more representative dataset.
  2. Address Multicollinearity: Employ regularization methods such as Ridge or Lasso regression, which are specifically designed to handle multicollinearity and produce more stable and interpretable feature coefficients.
  3. Advance the Modeling: Develop more complex, non-linear models, such as Gradient Boosting or Random Forests, to capture more nuanced relationships in the data and likely improve predictive accuracy.
  4. Complete the Research Question: Conduct the planned Survival Analysis to model the ‘time on market’ component, which will provide a holistic tool for evaluating real estate opportunities in Oregon.

Data Ethics

A commitment to ethical practice was paramount throughout this project, ensuring that our analysis provides valuable insights responsibly, fairly, and transparently. We addressed several key ethical dimensions:

Privacy and Data Handling

We have prioritized individual privacy by exclusively using property-level data and avoiding any sensitive, non-public information. While the raw data source contained owner names, this information was explicitly excluded from our analytical dataset and modeling process to protect the privacy of individuals. Our focus remained on the structural and transactional characteristics of the properties themselves, not the people who own them.

Algorithmic Bias and Fairness

We acknowledge that real estate data is not neutral; it can reflect and perpetuate historical societal biases, such as those stemming from redlining or socio-economic disparities. Our model could inadvertently learn and amplify these patterns. We took the following points into consideration:

  • Historical Bias: The sale prices in our dataset are a product of historical market forces. Our model, by learning from this data, may implicitly carry forward biases related to neighborhood demographics or historical underinvestment in certain communities.
  • Sampling and Measurement Bias: Our decision to handle missing data by dropping incomplete records presents a significant ethical consideration. If data is more likely to be missing for properties in rural or lower-income areas, our final dataset of 34,507 properties may not be fully representative of the entire state. This could lead to a model that is less accurate for these underrepresented groups, potentially creating an inequitable tool. Future work must address this with more sophisticated imputation methods.

Transparency and Responsible Use

Recognizing the potential for misuse, we emphasize that this model is intended as an informational tool, not as a definitive arbiter of property value or as a replacement for professional appraisal and financial advice.

  • Transparency: We are transparent about the model’s performance and limitations. With a Mean Absolute Error of approximately $92,834, the predictions should be understood as data-driven estimates with a known margin of error, not as exact figures.
  • Intended Use: The goal is to empower newcomers to the real estate market with better information. However, we recognize the potential for misuse, such as by institutional investors to make predatory offers or to accelerate gentrification in certain neighborhoods. Therefore, we stress that the model’s outputs should always be used in conjunction with professional human judgment and a qualitative understanding of the local context.
